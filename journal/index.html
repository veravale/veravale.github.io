Personal journal

The private archive.

These entries were written over years, not cleaned into a doctrine. Some are tender, some a little severe, a few embarrass me in useful ways. They are here because credibility is not only a polished biography. It is the record of a woman changing her mind with care.

May 2, 2026

A last note before I archive this season

I used to write these entries as proof that I knew what I was doing. That sentence is not flattering, but it is accurate. In the beginning I wanted a record. I wanted anyone who arrived here frightened and hungry to see that I had thought about the room before inviting them into it. I still want that. I am just less attached to sounding unshakable.

This spring I reread the early entries and saw a woman trying to be both brave and spotless. She wanted authority but apologized for the hand that held it. She wanted her husband to be honest, then flinched when his honesty had weight. She wanted to speak about race, comparison, jealousy, worship, humiliation, and the complicated grace of being chosen after not being centered, but she kept sanding the language until it was safe enough to publish.

I am less polished now, though perhaps better dressed. I believe more in fantasy than I did in 2018 and less in fantasy as rescue. It will not make an avoidant marriage honest by itself. It will not turn resentment into devotion. It will not give a man courage if what he really wants is for a woman to manage every consequence of his wanting.

What it can do, in the right hands, is reveal the living wire. It can make a husband say, very quietly, “I am afraid you will enjoy being free,” and then survive the answer. It can make a wife admit, “I do enjoy it,” without turning that admission into abandonment.

My husband is still my favorite witness. Not because he sees everything. Because he has learned not to demand everything, and I have learned not to mistake mystery for distance. There is still heat. There is also laundry. I recommend both, though not with equal enthusiasm.

April 6, 2026

The thing I no longer offer clients

I no longer offer instant reassurance. That may sound ungenerous, but reassurance can become a drug in this work. A man writes, “Is it wrong that I want this?” and what he often means is, “Please remove the cost of wanting before I examine the want.” I understand the impulse. I have had my own version of it, prettier perhaps, but not nobler.

Years ago I would answer too quickly. No, you are not wrong. No, you are not broken. No, your jealousy does not make you pathetic. All true enough, and sometimes necessary. But if reassurance arrives too early it can flatten the question. It can turn a charged moral and relational inquiry into a customer-service moment.

At home I learned this because my husband began to resist being soothed. He would say, “You are making me feel better before I know what I feel.” It annoyed me. I had built a whole feminine competence around reading the room and softening the corners. He was asking me to stop arranging pillows around the truth.

Now I ask better questions. What do you hope this fantasy will let you stop pretending? What part of your partner’s freedom arouses you, and what part frightens you because it is not actually under your management? When you imagine comparison, are you seeking degradation, clarity, devotion, punishment, or a holiday from being the center?

A man who can stay with those questions is usually ready to work. A man who only wants absolution may need kindness, but he is not ready for the sharper room. Not yet.

March 3, 2026

Marriage after the door opens

People ask whether exploration makes ordinary marriage feel dull. They imagine a door opens and then the hallway behind it becomes the only interesting place in the house. For us, oddly, the opposite happened. Grocery lists became more intimate. Long drives became more charged. His hand on the back of my neck in a doorway could rearrange an entire evening, precisely because we both knew there were rooms we had learned not to enter carelessly.

The secret was not escalation. It was attention. I know that sounds disappointing if someone came here looking for a ladder, but desire is not improved by treating it like a loyalty program. More daring, more extreme, more proof. I have watched couples exhaust themselves trying to keep up with the fantasy they claimed had liberated them.

There was a season when I thought we had to keep moving or the energy would fade. I did not say it that plainly. I called it “remaining open.” I called it “not retreating into fear.” He finally asked whether I was listening to the marriage in front of me or to an imaginary audience that might judge us for becoming boring.

I hated the question because it was accurate. Our marriage needed pauses. Not because the desire was false, but because the desire was real enough to affect everything else. We needed weeks where no one tested anything. We needed evenings where the most erotic choice was restraint and the most dominant sentence I could say was, “Not tonight. Come sit beside me.”

Eight years into hosting these conversations, I am less theatrical and more exacting. Fantasy can reveal devotion. It can also reveal avoidance, vanity, social laziness, and a hunger to be managed. I trust the heat. I just make it prove it can behave.

January 5, 2026

I no longer want to be endlessly understood

This sounds colder than I mean it. I do want my husband to know me. I want the ordinary knowledge: how I take coffee when I am tired, which silence means I am angry and which silence means I am trying not to cry, the exact hour in an evening when I become unreasonable if dinner has not happened.

But I do not need every part of my desire translated into something comfortable before it is allowed to stand. For years I gave too many footnotes. I would say something severe or hungry, then immediately explain the soft origin underneath it. I was not only helping him understand. I was protecting myself from being met as I was.

There is a little loneliness in being fully oneself. A useful one. Even in marriage, maybe especially there, a person needs a locked drawer. Not a secret life built from betrayal, but a chamber of selfhood that does not exist to be constantly audited. My husband has one too. I used to find that threatening. Now I find it attractive.

In our work around jealousy, this changed everything. If I am not required to make every desire instantly legible, then his jealousy does not have to be a trial. It can be a response. It can stand beside my mystery rather than attempting to confiscate it. He can say, “I feel the edge of not owning this,” and I can answer, “Good. Stay there a moment. You are not being harmed by the existence of me.”

I would not have written that sentence in 2019. I would have softened it. I am not softening it today.

November 18, 2025

The night the conversation stayed almost boring

We had planned a charged conversation and then both arrived to it tired. This is one of the least glamorous truths about long marriage: sometimes the body refuses symbolism. You can light the candle, pour the drink, wear the good blouse, and still discover that the most honest sentence available is, “I had too many emails today.”

In an earlier year I would have forced it. Not crudely, but with that polished insistence women sometimes mistake for devotion. I would have asked one more question, touched the subject from another angle, tried to pull the thread because I had already decided the evening should matter.

Instead we let it be nearly boring. We talked about a client pattern I had noticed, then about travel, then about whether we should replace the lamp in the hall. The charged subject passed between us twice, like someone walking outside a window. Neither of us opened the door.

Later, in bed, he said, “I liked that you did not make me perform my honesty.” That sentence stayed with me. Men are often accused of performing strength, but in these rooms they can also perform vulnerability. They can learn the right phrases, the right tremble, the beautiful submission that gets rewarded. A good guide has to notice when a man is offering truth and when he is offering a theatrical version of being good.

That night I did not guide. I let the marriage be plain. The plainness had its own authority.

October 11, 2025

The morning after is where character lives

Not the fantasy. Not the confession. Not the charged look or the carefully chosen phrase. The morning after. That is where character lives.

I have become almost strict about this because I have watched too many people treat the scene as the proof and the aftermath as housekeeping. They are wrong. The aftermath tells you whether the power had ethics inside it. Who makes coffee. Who asks, “How are you, really?” Who can hear yes, no, more, less, not again, not yet. Who resists turning one beautiful risk into a new requirement.

At home, our best repairs have been embarrassingly ordinary. A walk to buy bread. A hand placed on a knee at breakfast. A sentence returned to later because last night it was too hot to hold properly. Once, after a conversation where I had been sharper than intended, I folded laundry beside him until I could say the apology without decorating it.

Men who crave surrender sometimes want the morning after to disappear. They want the heat and then a clean reset, no residue, no responsibility, no conversation about how the fantasy landed in the nervous system. But devotion is not only how prettily a man kneels in the imagination. It is how well he returns to ordinary life without punishing the woman for what he asked her to hold.

If a man wants fire, he must learn how to tend the room after smoke. Otherwise he does not want a mistress. He wants a match.

July 23, 2025

The interracial conversation got more honest

We had to retire a few words. Not because desire became timid, but because sharper language made the fantasy hotter and cleaner. There is a difference between taboo and laziness. I wish I had learned that earlier, though perhaps nobody learns it early. Perhaps you need to hear yourself say the borrowed phrase and feel how little of you is actually inside it.

Interracial fantasy is never only one thing. It can hold admiration, threat, projection, history, shame, envy, worship, competition, and the ache of comparison. It can also become stupid very quickly when people let inherited scripts do the thinking for them. I have no patience now for fantasies that require someone else to become a cartoon so a couple can feel daring.

At home we spent months revising the language. Some words lost their charge once we examined them. Others became more precise. What stayed was the complicated electricity of difference, the fear of not being enough, the admiration of another man’s presence without reducing him to a prop, and the question my husband had been circling for years: would I still choose him after recognizing that the world contained other kinds of power?

The answer was yes. Not the comforting yes of “no one else exists.” A better yes. A yes with eyes open.

I am still learning here. I distrust anyone who claims not to be. Desire does not become ethical because we whisper it beautifully. It becomes ethical when we refuse to let heat excuse dull thinking.

April 26, 2025

On being a wife and not only a mirror

There is a trap in this work where a woman becomes the curator of a man's revelation. She listens, interprets, blesses, disciplines, forgives. It can look powerful while quietly making him the center of every room. I have watched capable women become priestess, coach, lover, judge, mother, and stage manager, then wonder why their own appetite has gone thin.

I recognized the pattern because I had lived a subtler version. My husband would bring me a feeling and I would turn it in the light. I was good at this. Too good. I could find the childhood thread, the erotic thread, the attachment thread, the aesthetic thread. I could make his longing feel dignified. I could also disappear inside the elegance of my interpretation.

One afternoon I asked myself, with more irritation than grace: where is my appetite when I am not performing competence? What do I want when no one is improved by it?

The answers were less polished than my advice columns. I wanted to be wanted without becoming a repair shop. I wanted authority that did not have to justify itself as service. I wanted the right to be moved, flattered, curious, impatient, amused, occasionally vain, and not immediately useful. I wanted a marriage where his surrender did not secretly make me responsible for all the weather.

We are better now. Not perfect. Better. He catches himself sooner when he tries to hand me the whole machinery of his feeling. I catch myself sooner when I reach for the machinery because it makes me feel necessary. A wife is not only a mirror. A mistress is not only a mirror either, though some men would pay handsomely to avoid learning that.
