Personal journal

The middle years.

This page holds the season where I stopped confusing momentum with depth. The posts are less tidy because I was less tidy. That is probably why I trust them.

February 12, 2025

The year I became less impressed by escalation

For a while I thought growth meant going further. More honesty, yes, but I mistook honesty for volume. I see men do this too: if the fantasy is powerful, the next step must be bigger, sharper, more undeniable. They arrive wanting proof that the desire has not stalled, as if restraint were failure in a nicer coat.

Then we had a season where the most charged thing was restraint. A private phrase before a dinner. My hand on his shoulder when a certain subject passed through the room. The quiet knowledge that he would tell me the truth afterward, even if the truth made him blush. I began to understand that anticipation can be an ethical practice. It makes adults wait long enough to learn whether they want the thing itself or merely the relief of having done it.

My husband was better at this than I was. He would say, “Let it stay bright for a week.” I wanted to accuse him of caution, but there was no cowardice in him then. There was discipline. He knew that some fantasies become smaller when rushed into logistics too soon.

Escalation has its place. I am not pious about this. There are doors that should be opened, arrangements that should be negotiated, exquisite risks that deserve real air. But attention has longer legs. It walks home with you after the scene has stopped performing for the imagination.

November 2, 2024

I apologized for enjoying the authority

This is embarrassing to read back, but true: I apologized for liking command. Not for misusing it. Not for being careless. For liking it at all. My husband looked at me as if I had apologized for having hands.

I had spent years distinguishing dominance from cruelty for other people. I could explain tone, consent, calibration, aftercare, repair. I could tell a nervous man that the woman who gives him instructions is not necessarily unkind. I could tell a woman that her appetite for authority did not make her a caricature. Then I would come home and soften every instruction I gave until it arrived wrapped in apology.

The change began in a small domestic moment. He had asked me what I wanted from an evening, and I answered with three precise terms. Where I wanted him to sit. What I wanted him to tell me. What subject he was not allowed to dodge with charm. Then I said, “Sorry, that sounds severe.”

He laughed, not cruelly, and said, “Vera, it sounds like you.”

That was the month I stopped confusing dominance with unkindness. Not all at once. I still overcorrected. I still made little jokes to dilute the current. But something had shifted. Authority did not have to prove it was loving every second. Love was already there, in the precision, in the restraint, in the fact that we both knew the difference between command and contempt.

September 19, 2024

The client question that followed me home

A client asked, “What if I only feel close to her when I feel a little below her?” It was not the first time I had heard the question, but it landed differently that day. Maybe because he did not dramatize it. He was not trying to sound wicked. He sounded tired of pretending equality meant sameness in every emotional room.

I told him what I believed: hierarchy in erotic life is not automatically a problem. The problem is unconscious hierarchy leaking into bills, childcare, contempt, resentment, or the right to be heard. Chosen asymmetry can be beautiful. Unexamined asymmetry is just laziness with perfume.

That evening I asked my husband whether he ever felt closer to me when he was not trying to match me. He took a long time to answer. Then he said yes, but only when he trusted that I would not use the position to make him invisible.

There it was again: the difference between being beneath and being erased. The men who come to me often fear their own softness because they imagine it will make them disappear. It is a woman’s serious responsibility, if she chooses to hold that softness, not to become intoxicated by how easily it can be bent.

I wrote that down badly at first. Too stern. Too polished. The more human version is this: if someone hands you his throat in language, do not confuse the beauty of the gesture with permission to forget the person.

July 16, 2024

A very small failure after a very good week

I pushed a joke too far. It was witty, which made me slower to admit it was unkind. There is no vanity quite like the vanity of a precise woman who has just landed a beautiful sentence in the wrong place.

He went quiet. I became defensive. We were boringly married for twenty minutes, which is to say: proud, tired, and bad at apologizing. The subject itself was not even dramatic. It was a small remark about his jealousy, the kind of remark that would have been delicious if invited and mean because it was not. Timing is not decoration in this work. Timing is ethics.

Repair was not dramatic. I said, “That was for my vanity, not our heat.” He nodded. We walked to the corner shop. Sometimes aftercare is an apology plus buying dish soap.

I am including this because people imagine a woman with authority is always in command of herself. I am often in command. I am also petty if hungry, vain when praised, and occasionally too pleased with my own blade. The work is not to become spotless. The work is to notice the blood before calling the cut elegant.

April 8, 2024

His jealousy had a face, and it was not ugly

I expected jealousy to make him smaller in a sad way. It made him attentive. Quieter. Bright around the edges. We did not go as far as either of us imagined that spring. The restraint taught me more than spectacle would have.

There had been a conversation with another man, not secret, not staged, not particularly remarkable to anyone else in the room. My husband watched me become more vivid under attention that was not his. I saw him see it. That is the whole sentence and also not nearly enough of it.

Later he told me the most powerful part was not imagining me beyond him. It was realizing I could enjoy his jealousy without despising him for it. I had to sit with that. I had been so busy protecting him from shame that I had not considered shame could be held like a hot cup: carefully, briefly, by choice.

That night changed my work. I stopped treating jealousy as evidence of immaturity. Sometimes jealousy is the body admitting that the bond matters. Sometimes it is an old wound. Sometimes it is possessiveness trying to dress as love. The art is not to flatter it or banish it. The art is to ask it for identification.

His jealousy had a face. It was not ugly. It was young, proud, frightened, devoted, and trying very hard not to grip what could only be held by invitation.

January 28, 2024

When I stopped making every rule sound enlightened

Some rules are about ethics. Some are about fear. The difficult ones are mixed. I used to dress all of ours in excellent language so neither of us had to admit when a boundary came from panic rather than principle.

Privacy was the first place this showed. I could make a noble argument for discretion, and much of it was true. I work with clients. He has his own professional life. Our marriage does not owe strangers a performance. But inside that true argument was a smaller, less elegant one: I was afraid of being known as a woman with appetite.

Once I admitted that, the rule did not vanish. It became cleaner. We still protect our privacy. We still do not turn our marriage into a spectacle. But the privacy is no longer pretending to be proof of purity. It is simply a container we choose because we like what can happen inside it.

This distinction matters for clients too. A couple can keep something private because it is sacred, or because it is shameful, or because it is nobody's business, or because they do not yet have the strength to withstand being seen. Those are not the same choice. They may lead to the same practical boundary, but they do not feed the same life.

December 19, 2023

A clumsy first rule list

We made rules over soup, which sounds less glamorous than it felt. Some were sensible. Some were secretly designed to keep us from feeling too much. I can see that now. Back then I called it prudence and underlined it twice.

The list had useful bones: health, sobriety, privacy, no surprises meant to test loyalty, no using erotic truth as ordinary ammunition. It also had fear wearing a blazer. “No ambiguity” was one of our rules. Imagine. As if desire was going to submit a quarterly report.

My favorite bad rule was “no emotional surprises.” I had written it with a straight face. My husband pointed at it and asked whether he should schedule his complicated feelings before or after lunch. I was annoyed because he was correct. What I meant was: please do not let the fantasy produce a version of you I cannot manage. That is not a boundary. That is a wish for control with lipstick on.

We keep a revised list now. Shorter. Braver. More room for human weather. The current version has a section titled, plainly, “What we do if one of us is more affected than expected.” It is less elegant than the original and much kinder.

I sometimes show clients the shape of that evolution, though not the private details. It helps them understand that rules are not a monument. They are living tools. A rule that never changes may be wise. It may also be a fear that learned calligraphy.

June 4, 2023

The night I stopped pretending curiosity was only professional

My husband asked if I ever envied the women I advised. I laughed too fast. Later, brushing my teeth, I admitted I envied their permission. Not their exact stories, but the fact that they had finally said the charged thing aloud.

It is strange to spend years helping other people name desire while treating your own as research material. I could talk about fantasy in private salons with wit and steadiness. I could help a man understand why comparison moved him. I could help a couple make language less frightening. Then I would come home and tuck my own curiosity behind professional composure.

He did not pounce on the admission. That restraint mattered. He just leaned in the doorway and said, “Then take a little permission.” Not all of it. Not a dramatic vow. A little permission. It was the most generous possible dose.

I remember the exact ordinary ugliness of the bathroom light. I remember the toothpaste on the sink. I remember feeling caught and relieved, as if he had opened a drawer I had been pretending was decorative. The good revelations never wait for flattering conditions.

That night did not make us reckless. It made us honest in a new register. We began asking what belonged to the work, what belonged to the marriage, and what belonged to the dangerous overlap where a woman finally admits she is not merely qualified to discuss the fire. She is warmed by it.
