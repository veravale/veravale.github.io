Personal journal

The first years.

The earliest posts are more cautious and a little over-explained. I leave that in. A backstory without awkwardness is just branding.

April 18, 2020

Lockdown made the fantasy domestic

The world got small, and so did we. Groceries, disinfectant, terrible sleep, the same two rooms, the same two bodies. Strangely, the fantasy did not disappear. It became less cinematic and more domestic.

Before that year, I still imagined certain desires needed atmosphere. A restaurant, a stranger's glance, a train platform, a black dress, some architecture of possibility. Then the rooms closed in and the desire remained, wearing slippers. It was humbling. Apparently the imagination does not require good lighting to continue being inconvenient.

We talked about what it would mean for a wife to have an inner life that did not ask permission every five minutes. We talked about men who want to serve but secretly hope service will make them indispensable. We talked about my impatience. We talked about his habit of dressing up fear as practicality.

No glamour that month. But a lot of truth. Some of it badly phrased. I remember telling him that I wanted more room inside the marriage and then immediately clarifying that I did not mean distance, did not mean rejection, did not mean danger, did not mean anything he might possibly misunderstand. He listened to the disclaimers and finally said, “You are allowed to want room before you have written the legal definition of room.”

I miss, a little, how carefully we listened because there was nowhere to run. I do not miss the fear. I do miss the way ordinary domestic life forced the fantasy to stop posing and tell us what it was actually made of.

November 3, 2019

Not a scene. Just a look.

There was a look across a restaurant table. That is all I will say, and honestly all that happened. But I learned how much can be carried without being performed.

I had been speaking with a man who enjoyed language in the way some people enjoy expensive knives. Not inappropriate. Not innocent either. My husband was at the other end of the table, involved in his own conversation, until he was not. I felt the moment he noticed my attention had changed. I did not turn away. That was the first quiet decision.

Later he asked if I enjoyed being watched enjoying the attention. I said, “A little.” Terrible lie. We both knew. The lie was not meant to deceive. It was meant to keep the room from becoming too bright too quickly. He smiled in that patient way that makes me want to either kiss him or throw a napkin at him.

That night taught me that a couple can begin with almost nothing. Not an arrangement. Not a confession with orchestral backing. A look. A delayed answer. The discovery that one person's attention can become charged because the other person is brave enough not to interrupt it.

I was still cautious then. I wrote in my notebook, “Do not confuse observation with permission.” Sensible. Also a little prim. I would add now: do not confuse permission with obligation. That sentence took me longer to learn.

June 22, 2019

My early mistake: treating jealousy like a spill

I used to clean up jealousy too quickly. In clients, in friends, in myself. I had this brisk little manner: name it, soothe it, regulate it, move on. Very sensible. Very bloodless.

That summer, my husband admitted he had felt a sharp pull in his chest when another man laughed too easily with me at a party. I reached for reassurance before he had finished describing it. He stopped me. Not dramatically. Just put a hand up and said, “Wait. I want to understand it before you fix it.”

I was embarrassed by how difficult that was. I wanted to tell him he had nothing to worry about. I wanted to make myself innocent, him secure, the evening smooth. Instead we sat with the feeling long enough for it to develop edges. It was not only fear. It was pride. It was admiration. It was the strange erotic clarity of seeing me as someone other men could notice.

That was one of the first moments I understood jealousy as information rather than simply alarm. Not all jealousy is noble. Some of it is possessive, punitive, childish, controlling. But some jealousy arrives carrying news: this matters, she matters, I am not asleep, I am not indifferent, I am being asked to trust something I cannot own.

I have repeated his sentence to myself for years. The feeling did not need to be punished. It needed a chair, a glass of water, and decent manners.

January 6, 2019

A winter note on permission

Permission sounds soft until you actually give it. Then it becomes a door with weight.

We talked for an hour about a hypothetical dinner, a hypothetical admirer, a hypothetical version of me who did not rush to make every feeling tidy. Nothing happened. Everything happened. I slept badly and woke up oddly proud of us.

The strange part was how formal we became. We were married, had seen each other ill and ridiculous, had argued about money and families and the correct storage of knives. Yet the moment the conversation turned toward my possible pleasure outside his centrality, we became almost courtly. Careful cups. Careful sentences. No one interrupting. It would have been funny if it had not been so tender.

I was still trying to keep the subject theoretical. I said “a woman” when I meant me. I said “some husbands” when I meant him. He let me do it for a while, then said, “Are we speaking about an article you might write, or are we speaking about us?”

I did not answer gracefully. I think I said, “Both, maybe.” It was enough. Early permission often arrives as an awkward maybe. I respect that now.

October 21, 2018

The first boundary that made me feel less impressive

At the third salon, a man asked whether his wife should tell him every detail if she ever explored her own desire. I gave a careful answer about negotiation, consent, and emotional capacity. It was a fine answer. Possibly even useful.

Then I went home and realized I hated the word “should.” Should she tell him every detail? Maybe. Maybe not. The better question was: what does he want the detail to do? Some men ask for detail because they want intimacy. Some ask because they want a blade. Some ask because they believe if they can hear everything, nothing can surprise them. That is not intimacy. That is surveillance wearing a silk tie.

I brought the question to my husband. We were not yet applying it to ourselves, not openly, but the air had changed. He said he thought detail could be a gift if freely given and a poison if demanded. I wrote that down immediately, which made him smug for the rest of the evening.

The boundary that emerged was simple: no one is owed every image. That sentence made me feel less impressive because it forced me to admit I had been overvaluing transparency. I liked the clean moral shine of full disclosure. It made me feel modern and brave. But privacy is not always avoidance. Sometimes privacy is how desire keeps enough oxygen to remain alive.

I did not know then how important that would become for us.

August 29, 2018

The first time he teased me about being too interested

I came home from a salon irritated in the way I get when I am pretending to be above fascination. My husband poured wine and asked three innocent questions, each one more irritating than the last, because he could hear that I was alive in the subject.

The group had been discussing men who are drawn to comparison, men who feel both fear and relief imagining a woman they love beyond their control. I had handled the room well. Calm, structured, precise. I had not handled myself as well afterward. I was buzzing and trying to call it fatigue.

“You are allowed to be curious,” he said.

I made a joke about professional research. He let the joke sit there, underfed. That was one of his quiet talents then: refusing to help me lie. Eventually I admitted that yes, I was curious, not about any one act or arrangement exactly, but about the emotional architecture. The way devotion and jealousy could stand so close together without canceling each other. The way a man might feel smaller and more loved at the same time.

He listened with a steadiness I did not yet know how to receive. Then he asked, gently enough that I almost missed the danger, “And do you want to be the woman in the architecture, or only the architect?”

I told him to stop being clever. He laughed. I changed the subject. The question remained.

May 17, 2018

Notes after the second salon

The second salon was smaller. Six people, rain against the windows, too much cheese, and one conversation that kept circling the difference between humiliation and devotion. I remember being worried that the room would become crude if I loosened my grip. Instead it became sadder and more beautiful than I expected.

One man said he did not want to be insulted exactly. He wanted to be seen wanting what he was embarrassed to want. That distinction has followed me for years. So many people use harsh language because tenderness would expose them more. It is easier to say “ruin me” than “please understand why this image moves me.”

I wrote too many notes that night. I always write too many notes when I am trying not to feel something. My husband made fun of the stack when I got home, then read one page and became quiet. “This is not about sex as much as I expected,” he said.

No. It was about rank, attention, shame, being chosen, being witnessed, being permitted to stop performing invulnerability. Sex was in the room, of course. It wore a dark coat and leaned against the wall. But it was not the only guest.

I did not yet know this would become my work in any serious way. I thought I was hosting conversations. Looking back, the conversations were hosting me.

February 11, 2018

Before I had a language for any of it

At the first little discussion circle I hosted, I thought my job was to keep everyone calm. Tea, chairs, a printed consent handout, my best composed face. A man in a gray sweater said he wanted to hear his wife desire someone else and then immediately apologized to the carpet.

I remember feeling protective of him and also privately startled. Not by the fantasy. By the apology. I wrote in my notebook afterward: people do not only hide what is ugly. They hide what is tender because tenderness can make them easy to hurt.

The room that evening was awkward in the exact way honest rooms are awkward before they become useful. Someone laughed too loudly. Someone used a phrase she clearly regretted the moment it left her mouth. I overexplained the discussion rules. The kettle clicked off at a dramatic moment and everyone pretended not to notice.

But beneath all that was a seriousness I had not expected. These were not people trying to shock one another. They were trying to find language for desires that had been living in them without furniture. A man wanted to be compared without being abandoned. A woman wanted to feel wanted as sovereign rather than merely reassuring. A couple wanted to know whether fantasy had to threaten their goodness.

My husband read one of my notes over my shoulder later and said, “That sounds like us, a bit.” I told him no, not really. I was wrong. I was also not ready to be right.
